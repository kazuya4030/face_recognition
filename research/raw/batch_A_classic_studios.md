## 1. Idealab (Bill Gross, Pasadena)

- **Overview**: Widely cited as the original/longest-running technology startup studio and incubator. Model = classic incubator/company builder: Idealab originates ideas in-house, recruits founding teams, provides seed capital, office space, and shared services, then spins companies out as independent entities in which Idealab retains a minority equity stake. [https://rossdawson.com/startup-studios/bill-gross-idealab-company-builder-successful/] [https://www.marketplace.org/story/2008/02/21/idealab-corporate-history]
- **History**: Founded 1996 in Pasadena, CA by Bill Gross, modeled explicitly on Edison's invention lab concept. First fund launched 1997 (~$12M investment fund, seeded by a $5M stake from ~10 investors including Steven Spielberg, Michael Douglas, and Compaq chairman Ben Rosen). [https://rossdawson.com/startup-studios/bill-gross-idealab-company-builder-successful/] In 2018 the incubator arm was rebranded "Idealab Studio," with Gross as Chairman. [https://labusinessjournal.com/la500-2025/technology-2025/la500-2025-bill-gross/]
- **Founders**: Bill Gross (sole founder), a serial entrepreneur previously behind GNP Loudspeakers and Knowledge Adventure (educational software, sold to CUC International in 1996 for ~$100M — enabling Idealab's founding capital). [https://grokipedia.com/page/Bill_T._Gross]
- **Funding**: Idealab itself has raised capital across multiple internal funds (Crunchbase lists 3 funds including "Idealab Angels I") but exact aggregate totals are not publicly disclosed ("obfuscated" in Crunchbase/PitchBook records). [https://www.crunchbase.com/organization/idealab] Confirmed disclosed figure: ~$12M first fund (1997). [https://rossdawson.com/startup-studios/bill-gross-idealab-company-builder-successful/]
- **Companies launched**: Over 25 years, Idealab evaluated more than 5,000 ideas, leading to roughly 150 companies founded. [https://x.com/businessbarista/status/1750243089602531460 — X/Twitter thread citing Gross's own "25 lessons" retrospective, cross-consistent with other sources] Crunchbase/Tracxn (lower confidence) count ~127 supported startups. [https://tracxn.com/d/companies/idealab] Bill Gross has said he has "advised and helped fund more than 100 companies." [https://review.firstround.com/lessons-learned-from-bill-gross-35-ipos-and-40-failures/]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: Sources converge on roughly 35–50 successful IPOs/acquisitions (First Round Review cites "35 successful IPOs and acquisitions"; other retrospectives round this to "50 IPOs and acquisitions," possibly counting differently over time). [https://review.firstround.com/lessons-learned-from-bill-gross-35-ipos-and-40-failures/] [https://x.com/businessbarista/status/1750243089602531460] Notable named exits: **Overture (originally GoTo.com)** — pioneered the pay-per-click search-ad model later used by Google AdWords; acquired by Yahoo! in 2003 for $1.63 billion. [https://rossdawson.com/startup-studios/bill-gross-idealab-company-builder-successful/] **Picasa** — sold to Google. **NetZero**, **CitySearch**, **Commission Junction** were also notable Idealab-launched companies. [https://www.marketplace.org/story/2008/02/21/idealab-corporate-history]
- **Success rate**: Bill Gross has publicly discussed "35 IPOs/exits and 40 failures" out of the companies he's funded/advised — implying a rough ~45-50% success rate among ventures that reach a definitive outcome (though total company count is larger, meaning many are still operating/private/unresolved). [https://review.firstround.com/lessons-learned-from-bill-gross-35-ipos-and-40-failures/]
- **Notable failures/shutdowns**: **eToys** — reached a $10B market value at peak, but collapsed and filed for bankruptcy after canceling its long-anticipated IPO plans post dot-com crash. [https://www.forbes.com/forbes/2002/0304/044a.html] **Eve.com** — cosmetics e-commerce site launched June 1999, shut down October 2000 with loss of 164 jobs. [https://review.firstround.com/lessons-learned-from-bill-gross-35-ipos-and-40-failures/]
- **Profit/revenue structure**: Idealab starts new ventures at 100% ownership but immediately transfers at least 50% to the founding team; provides initial capital (historically ~6 months of runway) and takes a minority equity stake, helping the company raise outside capital as soon as possible. [https://www.softtechventures.com/post/from-past-to-present-venture-building-model]
- **Competitive advantage/moat**: First-mover/brand as "the original venture studio," 25+ years of pattern-recognition and repeat-founder relationships, plus Bill Gross's personal reputation and network (Caltech trustee, ArtCenter board). [https://www.artcenter.edu/about/get-to-know-artcenter/people/bill-gross.html]
- **Hiring approach**: Historically Idealab generates ideas internally and recruits CEOs/founding teams to run them (rather than a pure open EIR-application funnel), leveraging Gross's own network and track record; specific structured EIR program details are not clearly disclosed in available sources — **unclear/not fully disclosed**.
- **Org structure**: Idealab Studio operates under Bill Gross as Chairman; the group has spun out its own AI-focused ventures directly (see below) rather than purely acting as investor. [https://labusinessjournal.com/la500-2025/technology-2025/la500-2025-bill-gross/]
- **Investment criteria / idea selection**: Not fully disclosed in sources reviewed — **unclear**. Historically Gross has emphasized picking large, timing-sensitive markets and testing ideas quickly/cheaply before committing capital (consistent with his well-known TED talk on startup success factors, "timing" being the single largest correlate of success).
- **AI usage (2023–2026)**: Idealab Studio has directly incubated **ProRata.ai**, an AI company. In September 2025, ProRata.ai raised a $40M Series B and launched **Gist.ai**, an AI search engine built on licensed publisher content, with partnerships including Time, Universal Music Group, News Media Alliance (Mar 2025), Boston Globe, Vox Media, and Future (Jun 2025). Bill Gross serves as CEO of ProRata & Gist and remains active in AI as of 2025-2026. [https://labusinessjournal.com/la500-2025/technology-2025/la500-2025-bill-gross/] [https://www.idealab.com/bio/management/bill_gross.php]

## 2. Betaworks (NYC)

- **Overview**: New York-based hybrid startup studio / accelerator / venture fund. Builds companies internally, runs a cohort-based incubator ("Camp"), and makes external seed/Series A investments — a "platform" model blending studio + VC. [https://www.builtinnyc.com/articles/rise-startup-studios-new-york-city] [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betaworks]
- **History**: Founded 2007 in NYC, based out of the Meatpacking District. Co-founded by John Borthwick with Andy Weissman (Weissman departed in 2011 to join Union Square Ventures). [https://www.recreations.media/p/betaworks-trilogy-part-i-a-builders] Launched "Camp," a 12-week cohort investment/incubation program, in 2016; WeWork invested in a physical "Betaworks Studios" space/clubhouse in March 2019. [https://techcrunch.com/2019/03/28/wework-backs-new-york-tech-clubhouse-betaworks-studios/]
- **Founders**: John Borthwick (CEO) — previously CTO/SVP Technology at Time Warner and CEO of Fotolog, one of the earliest social photo-sharing sites. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betaworks] Andy Weissman (co-founder, departed 2011, later partner at Union Square Ventures).
- **Funding**: Fund I: $48M (2016). Fund II: $46M (2020). Fund III: $66M, closed July 2025, focused on early-stage AI. [https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/22/betaworks-third-fund-closes-at-66m-to-invest-in-early-stage-ai-startups/] Separately, "Betaworks Ventures," a $50M seed/Series A vehicle disclosed in 2017, has invested in 150+ companies. [https://techcrunch.com/2017/08/10/betaworks-ventures-is-a-not-so-new-50-million-early-stage-fund/] Typical pre-seed/seed check size: $250K–$500K; Fund III plans ≥25 pre-seed/seed investments plus ≥50 investments via the Camp program. [https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/22/betaworks-third-fund-closes-at-66m-to-invest-in-early-stage-ai-startups/]
- **Companies launched (built internally)**: Giphy (acquired by Meta), Dots (acquired by Take-Two Interactive), Bitly (acquired by private equity), Tweetdeck (acquired by Twitter), Chartbeat, SocialFlow, Digg, Instapaper. [https://www.builtinnyc.com/articles/rise-startup-studios-new-york-city] [https://www.recreations.media/p/betaworks-trilogy-part-i-a-builders]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: Named internally-built exits: Giphy→Meta, Dots→Take-Two, Bitly→PE buyer, Tweetdeck→Twitter. As an investor (not builder), notable portfolio companies/investments include Twitter, Tumblr, Kickstarter, Medium, Anchor, and Gimlet Media, plus recent AI-era bets Hugging Face, The Browser Company, and Granola. [https://www.builtinnyc.com/articles/rise-startup-studios-new-york-city]
- **Success rate**: Not explicitly disclosed as a percentage — **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Notable failures/shutdowns**: Not specifically detailed in sources reviewed beyond general churn typical of an incubator portfolio — **unclear/not disclosed** which specific internally-built companies were shut down.
- **Profit/revenue structure**: Equity-stake model — Betaworks takes founding/large equity stakes in internally built companies and standard VC-style minority stakes in its fund investments. [https://www.builtinnyc.com/articles/rise-startup-studios-new-york-city]
- **Competitive advantage/moat**: Deep NYC tech-ecosystem relationships (15+ years), thesis-driven investing (notably an early, sustained AI thesis going back nearly a decade before the 2023 AI boom, seeding Hugging Face and Stability AI at pre-seed/seed), and the "Camp" cohort infrastructure providing compute/data-science support. [https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/30/betaworks-focuses-on-ai-applications-in-its-latest-camp/]
- **Hiring approach**: Recruits founders into "Camp" via open application (see betaworks.com/camp/application) for 12-week in-residence cohorts, rather than a classic 1:1 EIR-only model. [https://www.betaworks.com/camp/application]
- **Org structure**: John Borthwick as CEO/founder leads; company operates as a studio + Camp accelerator + multiple discrete funds (Fund I/II/III, Betaworks Ventures). [https://www.betaworks.com/team]
- **Investment criteria**: For AI-focused Camp cohorts, criteria centers on tools that "augment the way humans behave, create, play, work and think"; 2024 cohort selection fell into three categories — "agentic" B2B, personalized software, and hardware-enabled AI. [https://techcrunch.com/2023/04/13/betaworks-new-camp-aims-to-fund-transformative-early-stage-ai-startups/] [https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/16/betaworks-bets-on-ai-agents-in-latest-camp-cohort/]
- **AI usage (2023–2026)**: Betaworks has run dedicated AI-focused "Camp" cohorts every year since 2023: (1) 2023 AI Camp — $500K funding to ~10 companies, June–September 2023, with compute access from Hugging Face and Stability AI; (2) April 2024 Camp — 9 AI-agent startups; (3) October 2024 Camp — 10 "AI interface" startups; (4) Fund III (closed July 2025, $66M) explicitly targets early-stage AI. Betaworks frames its approach as "thesis-based investing," having built an AI thesis over nearly a decade pre-dating the 2023 boom (seed investor in Hugging Face and Stability AI). [https://techcrunch.com/2023/04/13/betaworks-new-camp-aims-to-fund-transformative-early-stage-ai-startups/] [https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/16/betaworks-bets-on-ai-agents-in-latest-camp-cohort/] [https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/30/betaworks-focuses-on-ai-applications-in-its-latest-camp/] [https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/22/betaworks-third-fund-closes-at-66m-to-invest-in-early-stage-ai-startups/]

## 3. Atomic (Jack Abraham)

- **Overview**: San Francisco-based venture studio. Unlike a typical VC, Atomic (also known as Atomic Labs) only funds companies it builds/spins up itself via an internal "Zero to One" team, rather than investing in outside startups. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/03/02/atomic-which-only-funds-the-startups-it-launches-just-closed-its-newest-fund-with-260-million/]
- **History**: Founded 2012 by Jack Abraham. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Abraham] Started with an initial $20M fund; raised a $260M fund (announced March 2021) and subsequently a $320M fourth fund (per Yahoo Finance/exclusive reporting), which added a former Postmates CFO as General Partner. [https://finance.yahoo.com/news/exclusive-venture-studio-atomic-raises-113000211.html]
- **Founders**: Jack Abraham — serial entrepreneur; founded price-comparison site Milo.com (acquired by eBay in 2010 for ~$75M) before starting Atomic. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Abraham] Andrew Dudum co-founded specific portfolio companies (e.g., Hims) alongside Abraham within the Atomic structure. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hims_%26_Hers_Health]
- **Funding**: LPs reported to include Peter Thiel and Marc Andreessen. Fund progression: $20M (2012 initial) → $260M (closed March 2021) → $320M (fourth fund). [https://techcrunch.com/2021/03/02/atomic-which-only-funds-the-startups-it-launches-just-closed-its-newest-fund-with-260-million/] [https://finance.yahoo.com/news/exclusive-venture-studio-atomic-raises-113000211.html]
- **Companies launched**: Atomic reported launching 14 startups in the 12 months prior to November 2021. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/talking-atomic/] Named companies founded by Atomic: Hims & Hers Health (NYSE: HIMS), Homebound, Bungalow, Found, OpenStore, Replicant, Exowatt. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/talking-atomic/] [https://www.forbes.com/sites/forbestreptalks/2017/06/02/jack-abraham-thinks-hes-found-a-better-way-to-build-businesses/]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: **Hims & Hers Health** — founded November 2017 by Andrew Dudum, Jack Abraham, and Hilary Coles inside Atomic; went public via SPAC merger with Oaktree Acquisition Corp on January 20, 2021 at a $1.6B valuation (NYSE: HIMS). Atomic retained a "material minority stake," reported at roughly 20-30%. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hims_%26_Hers_Health] [https://www.forbes.com/sites/katiejennings/2021/01/20/digital-health-startup-hims--hers-goes-public-in-16-billion-spac-deal/]
- **Success rate**: Not explicitly disclosed as a formal percentage — **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Notable failures/shutdowns**: Not specifically detailed in the sources reviewed for Atomic-founded companies — **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Profit/revenue structure**: Equity ownership model — Atomic co-founds and typically retains meaningful (reported 20-30%+ in the Hims case) equity stakes in the ventures it spins out, rather than charging fees. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hims_%26_Hers_Health]
- **Competitive advantage/moat**: The "Zero to One" internal team identifies well-defined market problems and rigorously validates ideas *before* recruiting/co-founding with outside entrepreneurs — reducing idea risk before capital and founder time are committed. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/talking-atomic/]
- **Hiring approach**: Runs "Founder-in-Residence" and "Engineer-in-Residence" programs; recruiting team reviews applications (rolling basis) for candidates who may fit an FIR/EIR role at Atomic itself or at an early-stage portfolio company. Process: initial screening conversation → interviews with key Atomic collaborators → a virtual working session with the core team. Program is intentionally "open" (pursue what you're passionate about) but structured enough to avoid isolated ideation. Atomic works with roughly 10–15 new founders per year. [https://www.atomic.vc/co-found] [https://johngannonblog.com/vc-fellowship-atomic-in-san-francisco-ca/]
- **Org structure**: Multiple General Partners (including a former Postmates CFO added for Fund IV) alongside Abraham; distinct FIR/EIR tracks and a dedicated "Zero to One" ideation/validation team. [https://finance.yahoo.com/news/exclusive-venture-studio-atomic-raises-113000211.html]
- **Investment criteria**: Focus on "well-defined problems" in large markets, testing and validating solutions rigorously via the Zero-to-One process before co-founding with the right entrepreneurial talent. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/talking-atomic/]
- **AI usage (2023–2026)**: Specific disclosures of internal AI tooling for idea validation were not found in the sources reviewed — **unclear/not disclosed**. However, Atomic's more recent portfolio companies (e.g., Exowatt, Replicant — an AI customer-service company) reflect an AI-forward investment thesis. [https://techcrunch.com/2021/11/17/talking-atomic/]

Gottesman and Galgon brought VC/exit experience (both ex-Madrona Venture Group), Gilbert contributed product/engineering background, Entress brought an extensive Seattle angel-investor network. [https://www.anthonybontrager.com/blog/why-we-invested-pioneer-square-labs-studio-3] [https://www.linkedin.com/in/greggottesman]

[https://www.geekwire.com/2023/seattle-startup-studio-pioneer-square-labs-raises-20m-for-third-fund-bets-big-on-generative-ai/]

Roughly 90% of ideas are killed before spin-out. [https://www.geekwire.com/2023/seattle-startup-studio-pioneer-square-labs-raises-20m-for-third-fund-bets-big-on-generative-ai/]

- **Profit/revenue structure**: Equity-stake studio model, plus a parallel traditional VC fund (PSL Ventures) that also invests in non-PSL-originated companies for diversification/returns. [https://www.geekwire.com/2023/seattle-startup-studio-pioneer-square-labs-raises-20m-for-third-fund-bets-big-on-generative-ai/]
- **Competitive advantage/moat**: Local Pacific Northwest network and reputation (founders ex-Madrona), a formal repeatable idea-validation pipeline, and corporate partnerships providing proprietary deal flow / problem sourcing with Fortive, FIS, Capital One, Rover, and Kroger. [https://www.geekwire.com/2023/seattle-startup-studio-pioneer-square-labs-raises-20m-for-third-fund-bets-big-on-generative-ai/]

[https://www.geekwire.com/2023/seattle-startup-studio-pioneer-square-labs-raises-20m-for-third-fund-bets-big-on-generative-ai/] Idea vetting uses internal metrics designed to mirror external Series A investment criteria before a "go" decision.

## 5. Rocket Internet (Samwer brothers, Berlin)

- **Overview**: Berlin-based global "company builder" / holdco, historically known for building fast-scaling replicas of proven internet business models ("copycats") in underserved international markets, then scaling them rapidly with heavy operational and capital support. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet]
- **History**: Founded 2007 in Berlin by brothers Marc, Oliver, and Alexander Samwer. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet] Prior to Rocket, the brothers built and sold Alando (German eBay clone) to eBay in 1999 for $43M, and Jamba! (mobile content) to VeriSign in 2004 for $273M — funding their later venture. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet] Founded Zalando in 2008, explicitly emulating Zappos.com. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet] IPO'd on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange October 2, 2014 at €42.50/share, valuing the company above €6.7B. [https://www.forbes.com/sites/ryanmac/2014/07/31/samwer-brothers-billionaires-rocket-internet-ipo/] Delisted from the stock exchange September 1, 2020 after prolonged trading below net asset value. [https://fortune.com/2020/09/01/rocket-internet-startup-incubator-germany-europe-tech-industry/] In April 2024, Global Founders Capital (GFC) — the Samwer-linked VC firm — formally became Rocket Internet's dedicated venture arm, deploying Rocket's own balance sheet capital instead of raising a third outside fund. [https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/26/global-founders-capital-becomes-rocket-internets-venture-arm/]
- **Founders**: Marc, Oliver, and Alexander Samwer. Oliver Samwer remains CEO. [https://www.forbes.com/profile/oliver-samwer/]
- **Funding**: Pre-IPO, raised $445M from Philippine Long Distance Telephone Co. and a further $582M from United Internet. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet] "Rocket Internet Capital Partners" closed a $1B fund in January 2017 — at the time, the largest tech VC fund raised in Europe. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Internet] As of the April 2024 restructuring, GFC had ~€300M on Rocket's balance sheet to deploy for venture investment (having previously raised two separate $1B GFC funds). [https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/26/global-founders-capital-becomes-rocket-internets-venture-arm/] As of May 2025, Rocket held a reported liquidity cushion exceeding €1.1B. [Note: figure sourced from a lower-confidence SEO-aggregator (matrixbcg.com); treat as indicative, not fully verified.]
- **Companies launched / portfolio size**: As of May 2025, Rocket Internet's portfolio comprised 164 companies, including 7 identified unicorns. [Note: sourced from a lower-confidence aggregator; directionally consistent with Rocket's well-documented scale but exact figure not independently corroborated via a primary Rocket Internet disclosure.]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: **Zalando** — Rocket's flagship build, now a major independent European e-commerce company (its own Frankfurt-listed public company). **Jumia** — African e-commerce company Rocket built; IPO'd on NYSE in 2019; Rocket sold its remaining 11% stake in April 2020 amid mounting Jumia losses. [https://technext.ng/2020/04/03/jumia-loses-major-investor-hours-after-lagos-govt-shut-down-its-warehouse-for-violating-lockdown-directive/] Rocket also retained/retains stakes in **Home24** (furniture e-commerce) and **Global Fashion Group** (online fashion). [https://weetracker.com/2020/04/03/rocket-internet-sells-jumia-stake/] Global Founders Capital's first fund is projected to return 3-4x, with **Pennylane** (French fintech, entered at pre-seed, now valued over €1B) cited as a standout winner. [https://techcrunch.com/2024/04/26/global-founders-capital-becomes-rocket-internets-venture-arm/]
- **Success rate**: Not disclosed as an explicit percentage — **unclear/not disclosed**. GFC's fund-level return multiple (3-4x projected) is the closest available proxy.
- **Notable failures/shutdowns**: Jumia's post-IPO trajectory has been repeatedly described as troubled (mounting losses, downsizing, exits from some African markets) though it remains listed. [https://www.tekedia.com/how-germanys-rocket-internet-built-and-exited-jumia-with-possible-loss/] Rocket Internet itself was criticized for years for its high-burn, market-share-first "copycat" model prioritizing growth over unit economics, which the company has since stated it moved away from (shifting to a path-to-profitability requirement within 18–24 months for new investments as of 2025). - **Profit/revenue structure**: Holdco/company-builder equity model — Rocket takes significant founding equity stakes in the companies it builds (via seed capital, shared back-office services, recruiting, and access to its cross-portfolio network), monetizing through IPOs, trade sales, and dividends/distributions from mature holdings (e.g., Zalando).
