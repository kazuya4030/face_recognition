# Batch B: AI-Native Studios, Talent Funds & Accelerators — Primary-Source Research

Research date: 2026-07-13. Compiled from official sites, founder/leadership interviews, careers pages, TechCrunch/Sifted/Forbes coverage, Wikipedia (cross-checked), Tracxn/CB Insights/PitchBook aggregator snapshots, and Hacker News. ## 1. Antler

- **Overview**: Global early-stage venture capital firm running a structured "residency" (originally "Antler Residency") model — a multi-week, in-person program where individual founders are recruited without a co-founder or idea, matched with co-founders on-site, and helped to validate and launch a company, culminating in a pre-seed investment decision. Antler describes itself as "the investor backing the world's most driven founders, from day zero." [antler.co/residency/us](https://www.antler.co/residency/us)
- **History**: Founded 2017 in Singapore by Magnus Grimeland and Fridtjof Berge. First cohort launched in Singapore in 2018 (1,400 applicants → 62 admitted → 13 companies formed). Expanded rapidly: Nairobi and São Paulo offices opened 2019/2022; by 2025–26 Antler operates in 27 cities across six continents, including a new San Francisco residency launched in 2025–26. [Antler blog "How we built Antler"](https://www.antler.co/blog/how-we-built-antler-2); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antler_(venture_capital_firm)); [Fortune, May 2026](https://fortune.com/2026/05/20/antler-ceo-magnus-grimeland-innovation-global-silicon-valley/)
- **Founders/leaders**: Magnus Grimeland (Founder & CEO) — Harvard College grad, ex-McKinsey, co-founder of Zalora (Rocket Internet). Co-founder Fridtjof Berge. Early partners included Gaute Andreassen, Jussi Salovaara, Vegard Medbo, Andrea S. Boehmert. [Forbes Business Council profile](https://councils.forbes.com/profile/Magnus-Grimeland-Founder-CEO-Antler/0528240f-0be6-45ae-af15-96a339b3654c)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: Closed $510M in new global funds in January 2026 (half earmarked for US founders). Regional vehicles include a $100M+ Antler Nordic Fund II. Total historical fundraising across dozens of regional funds is not consolidated in one public figure — **exact global AUM unclear/not disclosed**, but $510M is the most recent headline raise. [Tech Funding News, Jan 2026](https://techfundingnews.com/antler-510m-raise-us-expansion-unicorns/); [ArcticStartup](https://arcticstartup.com/antler-launches-new-residency/)
- **Companies launched/funded**: As of July 2026, Antler has invested in **1,538 companies**, with 91 new investments in the trailing 12 months (Tracxn snapshot). [Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/venture-capital/antler/__PtNIc9EeX2eEO1Uq12rJHvqdFnEIU7D_gH6v0WhzYP8)
- **Exits / notable outcomes**: 3 unicorns (including Airalo and Lovable), 2 IPOs, 20 acquisitions per Tracxn's July 2026 snapshot; a separate figure cites 142 total portfolio "exits" (incl. Leather, Earth3.0, Incito, Bounti — smaller/less-known outcomes, likely counting shutdowns/secondary sales loosely as "exits" in some trackers). [Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/venture-capital/antler/__PtNIc9EeX2eEO1Uq12rJHvqdFnEIU7D_gH6v0WhzYP8)
- **Success/failure rate**: Wikipedia cites that roughly "one in eight" Antler investments have failed — this is a low reported failure rate relative to typical pre-seed portfolios and should be treated cautiously (self-reported, methodology unclear). [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antler_(venture_capital_firm))
- **Notable failures**: Not individually well-documented in press; Antler does not appear to publicize shut-down portfolio companies. **Unclear/not disclosed** at individual-company level.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Standard VC structure — takes equity (recent US terms: ~$250K for ~9% common in the residency) plus follow-on capital from house funds; management fees/carry on the regional funds (typical 2/20-style VC fund economics, not itemized publicly).
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Global footprint (27 cities) giving geographic arbitrage and de-risked "day zero" sourcing before founders have ideas; large deal volume (1,500+ investments) creates a proprietary co-founder-matching dataset and alumni network across regions where competition is thinner than in the US/Europe.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Individual applicants apply into a location-based residency; historically extremely selective at the applicant→cohort stage (e.g., 1,400 → 62 in first Singapore cohort ≈ 4.4%; in one Africa market 5,000 applicants → 40 founders ≈ 0.8%). Investment decision comes only after teams form and build during the residency — most residency participants are not funded (see e.g. Nordic: "investment decisions in as little as two to six weeks" post-residency). [ArcticStartup](https://arcticstartup.com/antler-launches-new-residency/)
- **Org structure**: Regional "residencies" each run by local partners/investment teams under global leadership (Grimeland as CEO); Antler Nordic, Antler Japan, Antler US, Antler Asia etc. operate as semi-autonomous units with their own funds.
- **Investment criteria**: Emphasis on founder quality/drive over pre-formed idea; in AI specifically, some regional units (e.g. Antler Asia per late-2025 reporting) are reportedly avoiding generic "AI coding" startups in favor of domain-expert-led AI applications. [Let's Data Science](https://letsdatascience.com/news/antler-asia-avoids-new-ai-coding-startups-backs-domain-exper-b4f5cea0)
- **AI usage in own process (2023–2026)**: Reporting (via a 2026 VC-AI roundup) states AI is "proving to be extremely valuable for deal sourcing at firms including Antler" — implying use of AI for sourcing/screening at scale given their high deal volume model, though Antler has not published a detailed account of internal AI tooling. [Forbes Council piece / secondary roundup, July 2026](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2026/07/07/where-ai-agents-fit-inside-venture-capital-workflows/)

## 2. Entrepreneur First (EF)

- **Overview**: Self-described global "talent investor" — invests in individuals before they have a co-founder, idea, or company, running in-person cohort programs (historically ~6 months) to help solo talent find co-founders and build a company from scratch. Tagline: "Found, don't follow." [joinef.com](https://www.joinef.com/); [joinef.com/about](https://www.joinef.com/about/)
- **History**: Founded 2011 in London by Alice Bentinck and Matt Clifford (met at McKinsey in 2009; inspired by a McKinsey project on East London tech clusters). Considered the pioneer of the "talent investing" / company-builder category pre-dating most peers. Expanded to Berlin, Singapore, Hong Kong, Paris, Bangalore, and San Francisco over time (current active cohort cities per 2026 materials: London, Paris, Bangalore, San Francisco). Matt Clifford stepped back from day-to-day EF leadership in the mid-2020s (per Sifted) after ~15 years, while remaining connected to the firm; he also took on a UK government AI advisory role. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entrepreneur_First); [Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/matt-clifford-entrepreneurs-first-steps-back)
- **Founders/leaders**: Alice Bentinck and Matt Clifford, both ex-McKinsey management consultants, co-founded and co-authored the book "How to Be a Founder." [Wikipedia — Alice Bentinck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice_Bentinck)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: Raised $158M in June 2022 (Stripe's Collison brothers among backers) at a $560M valuation for the EF entity itself (this was a corporate/fund raise, not just a portfolio fund). In March 2026, EF raised a fresh **$200M** from a group including John and Patrick Collison, Eric Schmidt, Claire Hughes Johnson, Charlie Songhurst, Sara Clemens, Danny Rimer, Matt Cohler, plus institutional investors including Greylock. EF's portfolio is now collectively valued at **over $16 billion**, up from $3B in 2021. [TechCrunch, 2022](https://techcrunch.com/2022/06/28/entrepreneur-first-raises-158m-at-a-560m-valuation-adding-stripes-collison-brothers-to-its-list-of-backers/); [BusinessWire, March 2026](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260311135208/en/Entrepreneurs-First-Raises-$200m-of-Fresh-Capital-to-Unlock-the-Next-Generation-of-Outlier-Founders)
- **Companies launched/funded**: Not precisely quantified in these sources; EF has run dozens of cohorts across multiple cities since 2011. **Approximate/unclear** — Sifted's dedicated data piece on the EF portfolio (title only surfaced) likely has a precise count but was not fully accessible in this research pass. [Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/entrepreneur-first-portfolio)
- **Exit count / notable exits**: As of July 2026, EF reports **1 unicorn (Tractable** — UK's first computer-vision unicorn, insurance claims AI, customers incl. GEICO/Hartford) and **58 portfolio exits** (Tracxn). Other notable outcomes: Magic Pony Technology (acquired by Twitter), Sonantic (acquired by Spotify), plus acquisitions by Moody's and Facebook. Notable backed companies (not necessarily "exited"): Deliveroo (early EF-era company), Cleo (Balderton-backed AI fintech), Aztec (a16z-backed), Gensyn (a16z-backed), PolyAI (Khosla-backed). [Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/accelerator-incubator/entrepreneur-first/__qwN2Q-w4KGREuMYXPYvdSpAbFDvYppXE12CGt1lL5hU); [LinkedIn — Matt Clifford on Tractable](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/tractable-entrepreneur-firsts-first-unicorn-matt-clifford)
- **Success rate**: Not disclosed numerically by EF; portfolio valuation growth ($3B→$16B, 2021→2026) is the headline proof point EF cites rather than a survival/success percentage.
- **Notable failures**: Not documented in sources found; **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Equity-based — EF invests directly in the companies formed during its program (2026 US terms: up to $250K as an $125K post-money SAFE for 8% plus a $125K uncapped MFN SAFE, once the pair incorporates as a Delaware C-Corp mid-program). EF itself is capitalized by the $200M+ raises above, effectively functioning as an evergreen investment vehicle plus operating company. [Foundra guide](https://www.foundra.ai/accelerators/entrepreneur-first)
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: First-mover and strongest brand in "pre-team, pre-idea" talent investing; long track record (since 2011) generating trust among elite technical individuals; strong alumni/co-founder-matching network; access to marquee tech-founder LPs (Collison brothers, Eric Schmidt) which doubles as a talent/credibility magnet.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Solo individual application (2026 cycle is explicitly solo-only), heavily interview-based; extremely selective though EF does not publish a hard acceptance-rate percentage in the sources found. Selects on raw talent/potential rather than a business idea.
- **Org structure**: Multi-city cohort operation (London, Paris, Bangalore, San Francisco) under central EF leadership; described as a "company builder" combining fund + program operations in one org.
- **Investment criteria**: Explicitly talent-first — no idea or co-founder required at entry; selection is on the individual's potential to become an "outlier founder."
- **AI usage in own process**: No direct primary-source evidence found of EF publishing details on internal AI-driven deal screening. (Note: a "F/ai" all-AI accelerator backed by OpenAI/Anthropic/Google/Meta/Microsoft/Mistral was found in research, but that is run by **Station F** in Paris, a different organization from Entrepreneur First — should not be conflated.) **Unclear/not disclosed** for EF specifically.

## 3. South Park Commons (SPC)

- **Overview**: San Francisco-based (with NYC and Bengaluru locations) technical community + venture fund focused on the "minus-one-to-zero" phase of company building — i.e., before a company or even a validated idea exists. Self-described / press-described as an "anti-incubator": it invests in individuals and community rather than pitching a specific curriculum or idea. [southparkcommons.com/our-story](https://www.southparkcommons.com/our-story/); [TechCrunch, Dec 2021](https://techcrunch.com/2021/12/17/south-park-commons-an-anti-incubator-founded-by-early-fb-and-dropbox-engineers-gains-momentum/)
- **History**: Founded 2016 by Ruchi Sanghvi (Facebook's first female engineer) and Aditya Agarwal (former Dropbox CTO), following informal gatherings they hosted at their SF home after leaving Dropbox in 2015. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Park_Commons)
- **Founders/leaders**: Ruchi Sanghvi and Aditya Agarwal (co-founders); Prateek Mehta leads the SPC India Fund.
- **Funding raised / AUM**: Fund I (2018): $55M. Fund II (2020): $150M. Fund III (2025): $275M. Plus a $40M SPC India Fund (2025). Cumulative disclosed fund capital ≈ **$520M+** across vehicles (not counting undisclosed follow-on/opportunity funds). [VCSheet](https://www.vcsheet.com/fund/south-park-commons)
- **Companies launched/funded**: Not precisely totaled in sources found; SPC runs both a "Member Residency" (open-ended community membership) and a cohort-based "Founder Fellowship" (bi-annual, spring/fall). Notable/current portfolio companies mentioned in press include Render, Baseten, Luma AI, Imbue, Unit21, Profound, Goodfire, Doppel, Orb, Comun, Nuance Labs, Noble Machines. [southparkcommons.com/companies](https://www.southparkcommons.com/companies)
- **Exit count / notable exits**: Pilot (fintech/bookkeeping) was the first company founded out of SPC to reach unicorn ($1B+) status; its co-founders (described as "Waseem and Jeff") were both repeat/exited founders before helping found SPC as original community members. **Precise exit count unclear/not disclosed** in sources found — no consolidated public number located.
- **Success rate**: Not disclosed.
- **Notable failures**: Not documented in sources found; **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: VC fund economics — equity investment via the Founder Fellowship (2026 terms: **$400K upfront for 7% on a standard SAFE, plus an additional $600K guaranteed in the founder's next external funding round** — i.e., up to $1M total commitment). Management fees/carry on the ~$520M+ in funds. [blog.southparkcommons.com — SPC Founder Fellowship Spring 2026](https://blog.southparkcommons.com/p/spc-founder-fellowship-spring-2026)
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Dense in-person technical community (physical spaces in SF/NYC/Bengaluru) of accomplished engineers/researchers that pre-dates and out-lasts any single cohort — SPC's moat is the standing community itself (long-tenured "members" who aren't yet founding companies), not just a fixed-length program. Strong reputation among technical (esp. ex-Big Tech, ex-research) talent.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Rolling admission to general "Member Residency"; defined cohort admission windows for the "Founder Fellowship" (spring/fall). Uses an internal "Velocity and Acceleration" evaluation framework — explicitly does *not* require well-roundedness, instead looking for applicants who "spike" on distinctive traits and what unique contribution they'd bring to the community. Fast turnaround interview cycles around each cohort cutoff. [blog.southparkcommons.com — What SPC Looks For](https://blog.southparkcommons.com/p/what-spc-looks-for-in-applicants)
- **Org structure**: Community-membership model layered under a traditional VC fund structure; distinct India fund/team under Prateek Mehta.
- **Investment criteria**: Prioritizes individual trajectory/potential and community fit over a pre-formed pitch; strong bias toward applicants who can personally build/prototype if applying solo.
- **AI usage in own process**: No direct evidence found of SPC publishing internal AI-screening tooling; its own portfolio (Baseten, Luma, Goodfire, Imbue) is deep in AI infrastructure/model space, suggesting strong internal AI fluency, but a specific statement of "SPC uses AI agents to screen applicants" was **not found — unclear/not disclosed**.

## 4. HF0 (Hacker Fellowship)

- **Overview**: San Francisco-based live-in "founder residency" — a 12-week, in-person program (originally in a mansion/"hacker hotel," the Archbishop's Mansion) targeted at repeat/high-performing founders, explicitly built around minimizing distraction ("subtraction, not addition"). Operates as both an accelerator and a venture fund. [hf0.com](https://www.hf0.com/); [ASNA on the Archbishop's Mansion](https://alamosquare.org/2023/06/the-archbishops-mansion-the-hacker-hotel-incubating-the-a-i-revolution/)
- **History**: Established 2023 in San Francisco. Positioned explicitly around the AI boom from inception (SF Standard called it "the hacker house where workers eat, sleep, and breathe tech," Jan 2023). [SF Standard](https://sfstandard.com/2023/01/23/inside-sfs-most-competitive-hacker-house-where-workers-eat-sleep-and-breathe-tech/)
- **Founders/leaders**: Dave Fontenot, Founder & CEO. Previously co-founded Backend Capital, an early-stage fund with bets including Ramp and Fabric before it was unicorn status. [VCSheet](https://www.vcsheet.com/who/dave-fontenot)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: Fund progression: $8M → $16M → most recent (2025/26) **$100M** venture fund — a roughly 6x jump in fund size across generations. [Caplight / Everything Startups aggregation](https://www.everythingstartups.com/vc-funds/hf0-residency)
- **Companies launched/funded**: 10 teams per cohort (extremely small, curated batches); across "previous residency batches," HF0 states its founders have collectively raised **over $60 million** in follow-on funding. Early/notable backed companies cited include Ramp, OpenRouter, Doctronic, Krea (Krea AI grew to 20M+ users and announced a Series B after HF0), and ComfyUI.
- **Exit count / notable exits**: No IPOs/acquisitions specifically documented; success is measured by post-program fundraising, not exits yet (program is only ~3 years old as of 2026). In the most recent batch cited, teams raising after demo day hit an **average valuation of $82M**; 4 of 10 teams broke $2M ARR within the 12 weeks, and the top team went from 1 to 10M (metric unspecified — likely users/revenue). [How I Invest podcast, E236](https://howiinvestpodcast.com/episodes/zMfSQJxWOjy)
- **Success rate / selectivity**: HF0 describes itself as one of the world's most selective founder residencies — **only 10 teams chosen per cohort from thousands of global applicants, with an acceptance rate cited at or below 0.01%.** This is an extraordinarily low figure (lower than YC's ~0.6–1%) and should be treated as HF0's own self-reported marketing claim rather than an independently audited statistic. [cleverhack.com 2026 accelerator guide](https://cleverhack.com/2026-ai-startup-founder-resources); [Questd.ai](https://questd.ai/residencies/hf0-residency)
- **Notable failures**: Not documented in sources found; **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Up to **$1M in uncapped funding for a 5% equity stake** per team — a notably founder-friendly (low-dilution) structure relative to peers; underlying capital comes from the $100M fund's LPs, with standard fund fee/carry economics.
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Extreme selectivity + live-in "monastery of code" format targeted specifically at *repeat* founders (not first-timers), which differentiates it from YC/Techstars' broader intake; strong 2023–2026 timing alignment with the AI funding boom; personal brand and network of Dave Fontenot from Backend Capital.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Tiny cohorts (10 teams), primarily repeat/high-performing founders; described as "for repeat founders." Selectivity claims (≤0.01%) are self-reported.
- **Org structure**: Small, founder-led operation (Fontenot as CEO) rather than a large multi-partner institution; single physical site model.
- **Investment criteria**: Bias toward experienced/repeat founders building fast (AI-era) companies; emphasis on speed and focus over broad accessibility.
- **AI usage in own process**: No specific public statement found describing HF0 using AI agents internally for screening/diligence; however HF0's own portfolio and positioning is explicitly AI-native ("incubating the AI revolution"), and its extreme selectivity at scale (thousands of applicants down to 10) would plausibly require automated/AI-assisted triage, though this is inference, not a confirmed fact. **Unclear/not disclosed** as a documented practice.

## 5. AI Grant (Nat Friedman / Daniel Gross)

- **Overview**: A lightweight, remote-friendly accelerator/grant program specifically for AI-native startups, offering cash plus significant cloud-compute credits rather than a full in-person cohort program. [aigrant.org](https://aigrant.org/)
- **History**: Established 2017 (originally more of an open-source/research grant program) by Nat Friedman and Daniel Gross; evolved into its current startup-accelerator form as Friedman and Gross ramped up AI angel investing from 2021 onward. In 2023 they formalized a venture partnership, **NFDG**, with **$1.1 billion in committed capital**, and deployed the "Andromeda Cluster" — a private GPU supercomputer (2,512 H100 GPUs initially, ~$100M cost incl. power/cooling, growing to 4,000 GPUs by 2024) made available to portfolio startups. [Wikipedia — Daniel Gross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Gross_(businessman)); [DCD, on Meta talks](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/meta-in-talks-to-partially-acquire-vc-fund-nfdg-hire-nat-friedman-and-daniel-gross-for-ai-shakeup/)
- **Founders/leaders**: Nat Friedman (former GitHub CEO) and Daniel Gross (former Apple AI/ML director, Y Combinator partner, founder of Cue). Note: in 2025, Friedman and Gross were reportedly hired by **Meta** as part of its AI/superintelligence buildout, with Meta in talks to partially acquire NFDG — a major development for the future/independence of AI Grant that should be tracked closely for 2026 continuity. [DCD](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/meta-in-talks-to-partially-acquire-vc-fund-nfdg-hire-nat-friedman-and-daniel-gross-for-ai-shakeup/); [digidai.github.io analysis](https://digidai.github.io/2025/11/28/daniel-gross-ai-pioneer-fund-jerusalem-to-meta-superintelligence-deep-analysis/)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: AI Grant itself disburses **$250,000 on an uncapped MFN SAFE** per company, plus **$350,000 in Azure credits** and an additional **$250,000 in credits** (mix reported slightly differently across sources — consistently in the several-hundred-thousand-dollar cloud-credit range). This sits within the broader NFDG vehicle's **$1.1B** committed capital (not all of which is AI Grant-specific). [Skywork AI Grant guide](https://skywork.ai/skypage/en/The-Ultimate-Guide-to-AI-Grant-From-Open-Source-Funding-to-a-Leading-Startup-Accelerator/1976479960389447680); [Grantedai.com, Batch 4](https://grantedai.com/grants/ai-grant-accelerator-seed-stage-ai-startups-batch-4-aigrant-d5e6f7a8)
- **Companies launched/funded**: By 2024, AI Grant had backed **approximately 60 companies**. [Skywork AI Grant guide]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: No formal IPOs/acquisitions attributed directly to AI Grant, but its portfolio includes category-defining companies: **Perplexity** (AI answer engine — one of AI Grant's earliest bets, later raising at a $14B valuation by mid-2025), **Cursor** (AI code editor, fork of VS Code, since become one of the fastest-growing dev tools), **Pika** (generative video), **Granola** (AI notetaking). These are unicorn-scale outcomes on paper (not yet IPO/acquisition exits).
- **Success rate**: Not formally disclosed; the "collectively represent billions in cumulative valuation" framing from secondary sources is directional, not a rate.
- **Notable failures**: Not documented in sources found; **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Standard early-stage equity (uncapped SAFE) plus in-kind compute credits (Azure partnership — implying a Microsoft tie-in), sitting inside the larger NFDG fund's fee/carry structure.
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Founder-market fit is exceptional — Friedman (GitHub) and Gross (ex-YC partner, Apple AI/ML) have direct technical credibility and personal networks among the top AI research/engineering talent pool; the Andromeda GPU cluster is a genuinely differentiated hard asset (private compute access) most accelerators cannot offer; extremely high-signal, low-volume, high-conviction picks (Perplexity, Cursor) rather than a spray-and-pray model.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Lightweight/high-signal application (batches, e.g. "Batch 4" cited for 2026); selectivity rate not numerically disclosed but implied to be very high-conviction given only ~60 companies backed over multiple years despite Friedman/Gross's high public profile.
- **Org structure**: Small, founder-led (no large multi-partner staff structure evident); functions as an extension of Friedman and Gross's personal/NFDG investing activity rather than a large institution — this is itself a distinguishing "lean" model.
- **Investment criteria**: AI-native companies specifically; historically favored technically excellent founder-builders (engineering-led bets) over more commercially-packaged pitches.
- **AI usage in own process**: No specific public disclosure found of AI Grant using AI agents for its own screening/diligence pipeline; however given the founders' technical backgrounds and small team, informal/heavy personal use of AI tools in evaluation is highly plausible but **not documented — unclear/not disclosed**.

## 6. OpenAI Startup Fund

- **Overview**: OpenAI's corporate venture arm, investing in early-stage startups (many, but not exclusively, building on OpenAI's models) across healthcare, law, education, energy, infrastructure, and science. Operates with some independence from OpenAI core using deal-by-deal SPV structures alongside direct fund capital. [openai.fund](https://www.openai.fund/); [DevX](https://www.devx.com/daily-news/openai-startup-fund-leadership-shifts-to-ian-hathaway/)
- **History**: Founded 2021, based in San Francisco. Initial fundraise of **$175.25M** in 2021 (some reporting rounds this to "$175M"). Launched its flagship acceleration program **Converge** in December 2022, originally a 6-week (later described as 5-week) program giving ~10 founding teams $1M in equity investment each plus early API access; **Converge 2** and further cohorts have followed. In April 2024, Sam Altman stepped down as general partner of the fund (amid broader OpenAI governance changes), and **Ian Hathaway** — an economist and former investor at seed fund Haystack, involved with the fund since inception — took over fund leadership; **Brad Lightcap** is also part of the fund's leadership team. [Axios, April 2024](https://www.axios.com/2024/04/01/sam-altman-openai-startup-fund); [Global Venturing — Powerlist 2025: Ian Hathaway](https://globalventuring.com/corporate/awards/powerlist-2025-ian-hathaway/); [OpenAI Startup Fund — Introducing Converge](https://openai.fund/news/introducing-converge)
- **Founders/leaders**: Originally fronted by Sam Altman as GP; now led by Ian Hathaway with Brad Lightcap. OpenAI itself closed a **record $122 billion funding round** in Q1 2026 amid anticipation of an eventual IPO — separate from the Startup Fund but relevant context for its capital backing. [CNBC, March 2026](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/31/openai-funding-round-ipo.html)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: Reports vary: PitchBook/aggregator data show the fund has raised **approximately $114.2M across five SPVs** (as of Dec 2024) on top of the original $175.25M 2021 raise — total disclosed capital in the **high hundreds of millions**, not a single clean "AUM" figure; **precise current AUM unclear/not disclosed** (some corporate VC funds do not publish consolidated AUM). [VCSheet](https://www.vcsheet.com/fund/openai-startup-fund)
- **Companies launched/funded**: CB Insights/PitchBook snapshots differ: one shows **45 investments** (latest: Merge Labs, Jan 15 2026 seed round) while PitchBook lists **37** — reflecting different cutoff dates/methodologies. An earlier CB Insights snapshot described a smaller portfolio of 14 companies (stale data). Investment stage breakdown (CB Insights): 5 Series B (avg $161M round size), 4 Series A (avg $25.4M), 3 Seed (avg $15M), 1 Series C (avg $243M), 1 Series D (avg $5M) — 14 in Software, 14 in Tech broadly, 11 Enterprise/B2B, at least 4 explicitly "AI-Native" tagged. [CB Insights](https://www.cbinsights.com/investor/openai-startup-fund); [Tracxn](https://tracxn.com/d/venture-capital/openaistartupfund/__Rp6ugduZ613B4Hvba-352ySiK4qzAdIHtQFddJJlJW0)
- **Exit count / notable exits**: CB Insights reports **5 unicorns and 1 acquisition** in the portfolio, including **Speak, Harvey, and Ambience** (Ambience Healthcare) as key names. No IPOs from the fund's own portfolio documented yet.
- **Success rate**: Not disclosed.
- **Notable failures**: Not documented in sources found; **unclear/not disclosed**.
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Direct equity investment (fund invests OpenAI/partner capital into portfolio companies); Converge program specifically gives **$1M equity investment** per team plus early API/model access — the "currency" beyond cash is privileged access to OpenAI's models, which is a distinctive profit/value-creation lever (portfolio company success indirectly reinforces OpenAI platform adoption).
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Unmatched access to frontier OpenAI models/compute ahead of general availability; direct line to OpenAI's own research and product teams; extremely strong platform/brand pull for AI-native founders; deal-by-deal SPV flexibility avoids being bound to fixed fund-cycle constraints.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Converge program is described as "highly selective," 5-week format, ~6 hours/week commitment, final week in-person in SF; open to "founders from all backgrounds" — prior AI experience explicitly **not required**. First cohort ~10 founding teams. [Converge program page](https://openai.fund/news/introducing-converge); [TechCrunch, 2022 original announcement](https://techcrunch.com/2022/11/02/openai-will-give-roughly-ten-ai-startups-1m-each-and-early-access-to-its-systems/)
- **Org structure**: Operates "inside OpenAI" rather than as a fully standalone firm, per VCSheet — small dedicated investing team (Hathaway, Lightcap) rather than a large multi-partner bench.
- **Investment criteria**: AI-native / AI-forward companies across verticals (health, law, education, energy, infra, science) — a thesis explicitly aligned with OpenAI's broader "AI for everything" platform strategy.
- **AI usage in own process**: The **Converge program itself is explicitly about founders "using AI to reimagine products and industries"** — i.e., OpenAI Startup Fund's core programmatic bet is that founders should be building with AI/agents from day one, and portfolio companies get early access to OpenAI's own frontier models to do so. This is the most direct "AI-native by design" example among all nine organizations researched, though it is about portfolio companies' product-building rather than confirmed evidence of OpenAI Startup Fund using AI agents internally for its own deal screening/diligence (which was not separately documented in sources found).

## 7. Y Combinator (YC)

- **Overview**: The original modern startup accelerator — twice-yearly (now four-times-yearly) cohort program providing seed funding, mentorship, and a Demo Day culminating pitch event; widely regarded as the most influential accelerator brand globally and increasingly AI-saturated in its own batch composition and internal operations. [ycombinator.com/people](https://www.ycombinator.com/people)
- **History**: Founded March 11, 2005 by Paul Graham, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell, pioneering the batch-cohort accelerator model. Sam Altman became president in 2014, introducing a standardized $150K-for-7% deal. In January 2022 the deal was revised to **$500,000 total** ($125K for 7% equity + $375K via an uncapped MFN SAFE). **Garry Tan** became CEO and President in January 2023 (former YC partner 2011–2015, co-founder of Initialized Capital); under Tan, YC discontinued its YC Continuity growth fund. Since 2025, YC runs **four batches per year** (up from two), with smaller, more selective cohorts of roughly 140–200 companies. As of 2026, YC has invested in **over 5,668 companies** with a combined valuation of **$600 billion**. Next Demo Day (W26) was March 24, 2026. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y_Combinator); [TurboFund YC history](https://turbofund.io/accelerators/yc-history)
- **Founders/leaders**: Paul Graham, Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, Trevor Blackwell (co-founders); Sam Altman (president 2014–2019); Garry Tan (CEO/President since Jan 2023). Current partners cited include Diana Hu, Harj Taggar, Pete Koomen, Jared Friedman alongside Tan. [MarkTechPost / YC AI-native coverage](https://www.marktechpost.com/2026/05/22/a-step-by-step-coding-tutorial-to-implement-gbrain-the-self-wiring-memory-layer-built-by-y-combinators-garry-tan-for-ai-agents/)
- **Funding raised / AUM**: YC itself is funded by its partners/LPs (not a single public "AUM" figure comparable to a traditional fund; historically funded off Y Combinator's own fund vehicles plus, previously, the now-discontinued Continuity growth fund). Per-company check size is currently **$500,000 standard deal** (see above).
- **Companies launched/funded**: **Over 5,668 companies** funded since 2005 (2026 figure), combined valuation ~$600B. Cohort sizes now ~140–200 per batch across 4 batches/year (previously larger, less frequent batches). [TurboFund]
- **Exit count / notable exits**: Extremely large alumni list including Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, Coinbase, DoorDash, Instacart, Reddit (notable historical IPOs/major exits, well-established prior knowledge, consistent with public record). Recent 2025–26 note: over the last 15 years and 30 batches, YC invested in 2,200+ companies with **more than 400 officially inactive** (shut down); for the earliest 17 batches specifically, the inactive rate is roughly **40%**. Separately, YC has backed **500+ fintech startups** over 20 years, of which "the majority ultimately shut down" per a March 2026 analysis. [Failory / HN discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=36390737); [Crowdfund Insider, March 2026](https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2026/03/270081-y-combinator-invested-in-500-fintech-startups-majority-have-failed-analysis/)
- **Success rate**: Roughly 40% "inactive" (failed/shut down) rate for its earliest batches per the above — implying a ~60% multi-year survival rate for those cohorts, though "survival" ≠ "success" (most survivors are not high-growth outcomes).
- **Notable failures**: **Homejoy** — once called by Paul Graham "the fastest growing YC company," shut down July 31, 2015, due to high customer acquisition cost combined with poor retention (only ~25% of users returned after month 1). [Wikipedia — Homejoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homejoy); [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=10451143)
- **Revenue/profit structure**: Standard equity-for-cash accelerator model ($500K for combination of priced equity + uncapped SAFE); no consulting fees charged to founders.
- **Competitive advantage / moat**: Unmatched brand density and alumni network (Airbnb/Stripe/Dropbox-caliber alumni), deepest and most battle-tested startup-advice content library, strongest fundraising signal to outside VCs ("YC-backed" as a trust shortcut), highest applicant volume (tens of thousands per cycle) giving it the largest selection funnel of any accelerator in this set.
- **Hiring/selection approach**: Massive application volume — 20,000–40,000+ applications per cycle; acceptance rates have fallen sharply, with **Summer 2025 at 0.6%** (lowest on record) and Winter 2026 funding 196 companies at an estimated ~1% rate. Applications go into a shared pool reviewed by **15 equal investing partners**, who watch founder videos and try product demos as part of screening. [wearefounders.uk](https://www.wearefounders.uk/y-combinator-acceptance-rate-2026/); [YC application process description]
- **Org structure**: Flat partnership of ~15 "equal partners" on the investing side plus program/operations staff; CEO/President Garry Tan sits atop the organization.
- **Investment criteria**: Historically idea/team-agnostic but favors technical founders and fast-moving execution; **2026 batches are roughly 60% AI companies** (up from 40% in 2024); across recent batches (F25/W26/S26/Su26) about 49% of company one-liners contain "AI," "agent," "copilot," or "LLM," with 22% specifically referencing agentic AI. [TLDL YC AI batch breakdown](https://www.tldl.io/blog/yc-ai-startups-2026)
- **AI usage in own process**: This is the deepest and best-documented case among all nine orgs. YC leadership (Tan, plus partners Pete Koomen, Diana Hu, Harj Taggar, Jared Friedman) has explicitly stated **YC has transformed itself into an "AI-native organization"** over the past year, with internal infrastructure built around AI — Koomen describes AI as "a building layer for everything," emphasizing recording/learning from all internal artifacts to build "collective organizational intelligence." Garry Tan personally returned to hands-on coding after a 13-year hiatus and built and uses: **Gary's List** (a blog platform with agentic research features), **GStack** (a Claude Code skill library), and **GBrain** (a personal AI system built on "OpenClaw" with RAG) — all while running YC full-time, illustrating heavy personal AI-agent usage by YC's own CEO. However, direct confirmation that AI specifically screens/scores incoming applications (as opposed to partners using AI as personal productivity tools) was **not found** in these sources — the 15 partners still manually review videos/demos. [StartupHub.ai](https://www.startuphub.ai/ai-news/artificial-intelligence/2026/y-combinator-partners-on-building-superintelligence); [MarkTechPost, GBrain tutorial](https://www.marktechpost.com/2026/05/22/a-step-by-step-coding-tutorial-to-implement-gbrain-the-self-wiring-memory-layer-built-by-y-combinators-garry-tan-for-ai-agents/); [Vanta — Garry Tan on AI-built unicorns](https://www.vanta.com/resources/why-the-next-unicorns-are-built-by-ai)

## Cross-cutting observations (AI-era screening, for report synthesis)

- **Y Combinator** is the only organization in this set with strong, specific, sourced evidence of leadership (CEO Garry Tan) personally building and using AI-agent tooling (GBrain, GStack, Gary's List) and publicly framing the org as "AI-native," though evidence stops short of confirming AI directly scores/ranks applications — human partners still review videos/demos.
- **OpenAI Startup Fund** is the most AI-native *by design* of the group: its flagship Converge program's entire premise is founders building with AI/agents, backed by privileged frontier-model access — but this is about portfolio-company AI usage, not confirmed internal deal-screening AI.
- For **Antler**, a third-party VC-AI trend roundup (Forbes Council, July 2026) explicitly names Antler as a firm where "AI is proving extremely valuable for deal sourcing," but no Antler-authored primary source elaborates on this.
- For **South Park Commons, HF0, AI Grant, Techstars, 500 Global, and Entrepreneur First**, no primary-source or reputable secondary-source documentation was found describing specific internal AI-agent usage for screening, diligence, idea generation, or MVP-building — these should be marked **"unclear/not disclosed"** in the report rather than assumed. This absence of disclosure is itself a notable finding: most of these organizations talk publicly about AI as an *investment thesis* (what portfolio companies build) far more than as an *operating practice* (how the org itself works), except YC and OpenAI Startup Fund.
- Fund-size / selectivity outlier: **HF0's self-reported ≤0.01% acceptance rate** is far more selective than YC's already-tightening ~0.6–1% rate — worth flagging in the report as an unusually extreme (and unverified/self-reported) claim.
